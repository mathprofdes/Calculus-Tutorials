Infinite Limits#

Discussion & Definitions#

It is possible that when x gets close to a the functional values of \(f(x)\) either increase without bound or decrease without bound. That is, the graph of the function goes straight up or straight down. These limits do not exist either but we denote them a little differently to give the reader more information about the behavior of the function close to the value a. These definitions are still intuitive but are sufficient for most casess.

Definition: Infinite Limits

  • Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined at all values in an open interval \((c, a)\) with \(c < a\). If the values of the function \(f(x)\) increase without bound as x approaches a, with \(x < a\), we say that the limit as x approaches a from the left is positive infinity. Symbolically we write,

\[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \infty\]

  • Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined at all values in an open interval \((a, c)\) with \(c > a\). If the values of the function \(f(x)\) increase without bound as x approaches a, with \(x > a\), we say that the limit as x approaches a from the right is positive infinity. Symbolically we write,

\[\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \infty\]

  • Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined at all values in an open interval \((c, a)\) with \(c < a\). If the values of the function \(f(x)\) decrease without bound as x approaches a, with \(x < a\), we say that the limit as x approaches a from the left is negative infinity. Symbolically we write,

\[\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = -\infty\]

  • Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined at all values in an open interval \((a, c)\) with \(c > a\). If the values of the function \(f(x)\) decrease without bound as x approaches a, with \(x > a\), we say that the limit as x approaches a from the right is negative infinity. Symbolically we write,

\[\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = -\infty\]

  • Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined at all values in an open interval containing the number a, except for a itself. If the values of the function \(f(x)\) increase without bound as x approaches a from either side, we say that the limit as x approaches a is positive infinity. Symbolically we write,

\[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty\]

  • Let \(f(x)\) be a function defined at all values in an open interval containing the number a, except for a itself. If the values of the function \(f(x)\) decrease without bound as x approaches a from either side, we say that the limit as x approaches a is negative infinity. Symbolically we write,

\[\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = -\infty\]

Positive infinity (\(\infty\)) and negative infinity (\(-\infty\)) are not real numbers. So in all the cases above, the limit does not exist. We write \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = -\infty\) (instead of DNE) to indicate how the limit does not exist and subsequently give us more information about the behavior of the function.

Definition: Vertical Asymptote

Let \(f(x)\) be a function. If any of the following conditions hold, then the line \(x = a\) is a vertical asymptote of \(f(x)\).

\[\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \pm\infty, \quad \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \pm\infty, \quad {\rm or} \quad \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \pm\infty\]

where \(\pm\infty\) means either \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\).

Example: \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\)#

For this example we are interested in what is happening close to \(x = 0\).

Numeric Approach#

If we attack this numerically we can see that the functional values get very large (positively and negatively) as \(x \to 0\).

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{l|l} x & \frac{1}{x} \\ \hline 1 & 1 \\ 0.1 & 10.0 \\ 0.01 & 100.0 \\ 0.001 & 1000.0 \\ 0.0001 & 10000.0 \\ 1.0 \cdot 10^{-5} & 100000.0 \\ 1.0 \cdot 10^{-6} & 1000000.0 \\ 1.0 \cdot 10^{-7} & 10000000.0 \\ 1.0 \cdot 10^{-8} & 100000000.0 \\ 1.0 \cdot 10^{-9} & 1000000000.0 \\ \end{array}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{l|l} x & \frac{1}{x} \\ \hline -1 & -1 \\ -0.1 & -10.0 \\ -0.01 & -100.0 \\ -0.001 & -1000.0 \\ -0.0001 & -10000.0 \\ -1.0 \cdot 10^{-5} & -100000.0 \\ -1.0 \cdot 10^{-6} & -1000000.0 \\ -1.0 \cdot 10^{-7} & -10000000.0 \\ -1.0 \cdot 10^{-8} & -100000000.0 \\ -1.0 \cdot 10^{-9} & -1000000000.0 \\ \end{array}\end{split}\]

From the definitions above we would say,

\[\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = \infty \quad {\rm and} \quad \lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty\]

Since the one-sided limits are “going to” different infinities we cannot use this notation for the two-sided limit, all we can write here is,

\[\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x} \quad DNE\]

We would also say that \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) has a vertical asymptote \(x = 0\), or that \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 0\).

Graphical Approach#

Graphing the function \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) shows the same behavior as we saw with the numeric approach. As we approach 0 from the right the corresponding functional values get larger and larger, and as we approach 0 from the left the corresponding functional values get larger and larger negatively.

This gives us the same conclusions,

\[\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = \infty \quad {\rm and} \quad \lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x} = -\infty\]

Since the one-sided limits are “going to” different infinities we cannot use this notation for the two-sided limit, all we can write here is,

\[\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{x} \quad DNE\]

We would also say that \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) has a vertical asymptote \(x = 0\), or that \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = 0\).

Example: Einstein’s Equation#

Einstein’s equation for the mass of a moving object is

\[m = \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}}\]

where \(m_0\) is the object’s mass at rest, \(v\) is its speed, and \(c\) is the speed of light. From the equation it is clear that as v increases the denominator decreases and hence (if \(m_0 > 0\)) the mass of the object increases.

We will look at the situation of \(v \to c^-\) graphically and algebraically.

GeoGebra#

In GeoGebra the independent variable needs to be x and not v so we will use the expression,

m0/sqrt(1-x^2/c^2)

When you paste this into GeoGebra, it automatically creates sliders for m0 and c, with default values of 1. Although c is not 1 it is a constant so for our purposes 1 is fine. The image is,

From this we can see that

\[\lim_{v \to c^-} m = \lim_{v \to c^-} \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \infty\]

CLAE#

In CLAE the syntax for Einstein’s equation is,

m0/sqrt(1-v^2/c^2)

If we wish to graph this we first need to change the variable v to x. This can be done with Algebra > Evaluate, change the Variable to v and the Expression to x and hit OK. This will result in,

\[\frac{m_{0}}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{x^{2}}{c^{2}}}}\]

If we plot this CLAE will automatically create sliders for m0 and c, with default values of 1. Although c is not 1 it is a constant so for our purposes 1 is fine. The image is,

From this we can see that

\[\lim_{v \to c^-} m = \lim_{v \to c^-} \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \infty\]

We can also attack this algebraically in CLAE. Select the first input

\[\frac{m_{0}}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^{2}}{c^{2}}}}\]

Then Calculus > Limit, use the variable v, limit point c, and a direction of Left. The result will be,

\[\infty \operatorname{sign}{\left(\frac{m_{0}}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{c}}} \right)}\]

The sign function is simply a function that is 1 if the argument is positive, \(-1\) if the argument is negative, and 0 if the argument is 0. So the result depends on the values of \(m_0\) and \(c\). The computer algebra system in CLAE does not make any assumptions about the variables unless we tell it to. Since we know that both \(m_0\) and \(c\) are positive it is clear that this sign function is 1 and the result is \(\infty\). We could have told CLAE this in the limit calculation by doing the following.

Select the first input

\[\frac{m_{0}}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^{2}}{c^{2}}}}\]

Then Calculus > Limit, use the variable v, limit point c, and a direction of Left. Before we click OK, click on the button at the bottom, Edit Variable Properties. Another dialog box will appear with a list of variables on the left and a bunch of selections on the right. Click c and then select Positive, then select m0 and again select Positive. Click OK and the dialog will close taking you back to the first one and the types for c and m0 have changed. Now click OK and the CAS will display a result of \(\infty\).

Maxima#

In Maxima the syntax for Einstein’s equation is,

f(v):=m0/sqrt(1 - v^2/c^2)

Since m0 and c are constants and Maxima does not create sliders for constants in graphs, like GeoGebra and CLAE, we cannot graph this unless we substitute values for m0 and c. So if we let \(m_0 = 1\) and \(c = 4\) we get,

f(v):=1/sqrt(1 - v^2/4^2)

which we can graph. When you do make sure that the independent variable is v, let v go from 0 to 7 and y from 0 to 10.

Showing the infinite limit.

\[\lim_{v \to c^-} m = \lim_{v \to c^-} \frac{m_0}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}} = \infty\]

Example: \(f(x) = \frac{x^{2} + 2 x + 1}{x^{2} - 2 x - 3}\)#

We can have rational expressions where the denominator is 0 and not have a vertical asymptote there. Again, we will concentrate on this graphically. We will examine the function,

\[f(x) = \frac{x^{2} + 2 x + 1}{x^{2} - 2 x - 3}\]

GeoGebra#

To graph this in GeoGebra we use the syntax,

(x^2 + 2 x + 1)/(x^2 - 2 x - 3)

which gives the image,

From the graph it appears that,

\[\lim_{x \to 3^+} \frac{x^{2} + 2 x + 1}{x^{2} - 2 x - 3} = \infty \quad {\rm and} \quad \lim_{x \to 3^-} \frac{x^{2} + 2 x + 1}{x^{2} - 2 x - 3} = -\infty\]

and hence there is a vertical asymptote at \(x = 3\). It also appears that there are no other vertical asymptotes, and there are not. It is easy to see that the denominator \(x^{2} - 2 x - 3 = \left(x - 3\right) \left(x + 1\right)\) and hence is zero at \(x = 3\) and \(x = -1\) but there is no vertical asymptote at \(x = -1\).

Although we can factor this denominator in our heads, if we could not here is how you can use GeoGebra to do so. Select a new cell, run the command Denominator(f), this will extract the denominator and plot it, you should see the following. GeoGebra will also give the denominator a function name, say it was g(x). Now select a new cell and run the command Factor(g) and it will be factored into \(\left(x - 3\right) \left(x + 1\right)\).

The reason that \(x = -1\) is not an asymptote is because there is no infinite limit at \(x = -1\), which is caused by the numerator also having \(x = -1\) as a root. If we select a new cell and run Simplify(f) we get,

\[\frac{x + 1}{x - 3}\]

which does not have a root of \(x = -1\) in the denominator.

Note

The original function

\[f(x) = \frac{x^{2} + 2 x + 1}{x^{2} - 2 x - 3}\]

and its simplification

\[g(x) = \frac{x + 1}{x - 3}\]

are not equal. For two functions to be equal they must be equal at all values of the variable. These two are except at \(x = -1\) since \(g(-1) = 0\) and \(f(-1)\) is undefined. In reality there is a hole in the graph of \(f(x)\) at \(x = -1\), like,
