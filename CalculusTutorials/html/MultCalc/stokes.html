Stokes’ Theorem#

Discussion & Definitions#

Stokes’ theorem says we can calculate the flux of \({\rm curl}(\mathbf{F})\) across surface S by just knowing the values of \(\mathbf{F}\) along the boundary of S. Conversely, we can calculate the line integral of vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) along the boundary of a surface S by translating it to a double integral of \({\rm curl}(\mathbf{F})\) over S.

Definition: Positive Orientation

Let S be an oriented smooth surface with unit normal vector \(\mathbf{N}.\) Furthermore, suppose the boundary of S is a simple closed curve C. The orientation of S induces the positive orientation of C if, as you walk in the positive direction around C with your head pointing in the direction of \(\mathbf{N},\) the surface is always on your left.

Theorem: Stokes’ Theorem

Let S be a piecewise smooth oriented surface with a boundary that is a simple closed curve C with positive orientation. If \(\mathbf{F}\) is a vector field with component functions that have continuous partial derivatives on an open region containing S, then

\[\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S {\rm curl}(\mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}\]

Example#

In this example we will let \(\mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = (-x y^2, x z, y^2 z^3)\) and we will let C be the curve of intersection between the plane \(z = 2-y\) and the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=1\) moving counterclockwise if viewed from above. We will find,

\[\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}\]

CLAE#

First we will get a visualization of the curve C. Input

2 - y

and

x^2 + y^2 - 1

Click and drag both over to the 3D graphics window. Leave the first on as a function and change the second to an implicit plot. If we also change the second to a grid instead of a surface, and zoom in, we see,

By Stokes’ Theorem we can turn this line integral into a surface integral and we can use any surface that has C as a boundary. We will take the obvious surface of the plane used to construct C, \(z = g(x, y) = 2-y.\) The domain this integral is over is the unit disk. Input the vector field,

[-x*y^2,x*z,y^2*z^3]

Find its curl with Calculus > Vector Calculus > Curl, the result is,

\[\begin{split}\left[\begin{array}{c}- x + 2 y z^{3}\\0\\2 x y + z\end{array}\right]\end{split}\]

using,

\[\begin{split}\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} & = \iint_S {\rm curl}(\mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \left( -P \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} - Q \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} + R \right) \; dA \\ & = \iint_D 2 x y + z \; dA \\ & = \iint_D 2 x y + 2-y \; dA \\ & = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 (2 r \cos(\theta) r \sin(\theta) + 2-r \sin(\theta)) r \; dr \; d\theta \\\end{split}\]

Input the integrand,

r*(2*r^2*sin(t)*cos(t) - r*sin(t) + 2)

Now do the double integral with Calculus > Multiple Integrals > Double Integral, first variable r with bounds 0 and 1, and second variable t with bounds 0 and 2*pi. The result is, \(2\pi.\)

Note

Stokes’ Theorem allows us to compute a surface integral simply by knowing the values of \(\mathbf{F}\) on the boundary curve C. SO if we have two surfaces \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) with the same boundary curve, and the other conditions of Stokes’ Theorem are satisfied, then,

\[\iint_{S_1} {\rm curl}(\mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{S_2} {\rm curl}(\mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}\]

This can be used to simplify the integration by choosing another surface.
